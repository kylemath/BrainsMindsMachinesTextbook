Chapter Three

Chemical Conversations

How electrical signals trigger neurotransmitter release

Before diving into synapses, we need the essential electrical concepts from Chapter 2. Think of this as your electrical toolkit for understanding how action potentials trigger neurotransmitter release. We'll focus on three key principles: how neurons maintain their electrical readiness, how they generate all-or-nothing signals, and how these signals travel at lightning speed to synaptic terminals.

Essential Electrical Concepts: Your Synaptic Toolkit

Every chemical conversation between neurons begins with an electrical signal. Here's what you need to know about neural electricity to understand synaptic transmission.

The Resting Potential: A Loaded Battery

Neurons maintain −70 mV across their membrane—like a tiny battery waiting to discharge. This voltage exists because the membrane is selectively permeable to potassium (which wants to flow out) but restricts sodium permeability (which wants to flow in). The Nernst equation predicts equilibrium potentials for each ion, while the Goldman–Hodgkin–Katz (GHK) equation explains why real neurons settle near −70 mV—a compromise between potassium wanting to make the cell more negative and sodium wanting to make it more positive. This resting potential is the starting point for every action potential that triggers neurotransmitter release.

Action Potentials: The All-or-Nothing Signal

Hodgkin and Huxley's Nobel Prize-winning work revealed the action potential's elegant mechanism: voltage-gated sodium channels open rapidly when threshold is reached, driving membrane potential from −70 mV to +40 mV in less than a millisecond. Then sodium channels inactivate while potassium channels open, restoring the resting potential. This creates the absolute refractory period—a brief moment when no new action potential can fire, ensuring signals travel in one direction only. The beauty is that every action potential is identical in amplitude and duration, providing a reliable digital signal that travels without degradation.

Conduction Velocity: Speed Matters for Synapses

Action potentials travel at different speeds depending on axon diameter and myelination. Unmyelinated fibers conduct slowly (1 m/s) because the action potential must regenerate at every point along the membrane. Myelinated fibers achieve saltatory conduction—action potentials "jump" between nodes at 120 m/s. This speed difference is crucial for synaptic timing: fast-conducting axons can precisely coordinate with slower inputs to create temporal windows for synaptic plasticity. The action potential typically begins at the axon initial segment (AIS), where sodium channels are most densely packed.

Why This Matters for Synapses

When an action potential reaches the synaptic terminal, its precise timing and amplitude are crucial. The action potential must depolarize the terminal enough to open voltage-gated calcium channels—the molecular switches that trigger neurotransmitter release. The all-or-nothing nature ensures reliable calcium influx, while conduction velocity determines when different inputs arrive at their targets. Understanding these electrical fundamentals is essential because synaptic transmission is fundamentally about converting the reliable electrical signal of action potentials into the flexible chemical language of neurotransmitters.

Bridging to Chemistry: Voltage Hands the Baton to Molecules

Electrical signals provide precise timing and reliable propagation over long distances. Chemical signaling enables signal modulation, amplification, and integration. Voltage-gated calcium channels convert electrical signals into neurotransmitter release; postsynaptic receptors convert chemical signals back into electrical currents and trigger plasticity mechanisms.

Small voltage changes at axon terminals determine calcium influx and vesicle fusion probability. Synaptic transmission is probabilistic rather than deterministic, with typical success rates of 10–30%. This variability enables synaptic plasticity and prevents saturation. Tripartite synapses include astrocytes that clear neurotransmitters via specific transporters (EAATs for glutamate, GATs for GABA), buffer extracellular potassium, and release gliotransmitters that modulate synaptic strength. Acetylcholine is unique because it's cleared by enzymatic breakdown (acetylcholinesterase) rather than reuptake. These mechanisms convert electrical signals into chemical signals and back again.

Neuromuscular Junction: The First Chemical Proof

Otto Loewi's 1921 experiment proved that neurons communicate via chemical messengers, not just electrical currents. Loewi stimulated a frog's vagus nerve to slow one heart, then transferred the bathing solution to a second heart, which also slowed. This proved the signal was a diffusible chemical substance, which he called "Vagusstoff" (later identified as acetylcholine (ACh)). The neuromuscular junction became the model system: motor neurons release ACh, which binds nicotinic receptors on muscle fibers, generating end-plate potentials that trigger contraction. Curare blocks these receptors, causing paralysis. Bernard Katz discovered that neurotransmitter release occurs in discrete quantal packets corresponding to individual vesicles, establishing the vesicular basis of synaptic transmission.

Dale's principle originally stated that each neuron releases only one neurotransmitter. Current evidence shows co-release is common: many neurons release multiple transmitters simultaneously, such as acetylcholine with ATP, or even glutamate with GABA in certain circuits, enabling more complex signaling.

How Neurotransmitters Are Released: Calcium and the SNARE Machine

Action potentials open voltage-gated calcium channels at axon terminals, triggering vesicle fusion within microseconds. Synaptotagmin acts as the calcium sensor, while SNARE proteins execute membrane fusion: synaptobrevin on vesicles binds to syntaxin and SNAP-25 on the presynaptic membrane, pulling the membranes together. Clostridial neurotoxins revealed SNARE function: botulinum toxin cleaves SNAREs causing flaccid paralysis, while tetanus toxin blocks inhibitory neurotransmitter release causing rigid paralysis. Electron microscopy captured vesicles in various fusion states. Release probability varies between synapses and decreases with repeated stimulation as the readily releasable pool depletes.

Vesicle release probability typically ranges from 0.1–0.3 per docked vesicle per action potential. Calcium creates nanodomains near release sites that enable precise timing. Paired stimuli show facilitation when residual calcium from the first spike enhances the second release. High-frequency stimulation causes depression as vesicles deplete faster than they can be replenished. Vesicles can either fully fuse with the membrane or undergo transient "kiss-and-run" fusion depending on calcium levels and vesicle pool status. Endocannabinoids released postsynaptically activate presynaptic CB1 receptors to suppress neurotransmitter release, providing rapid negative feedback control.

Fast Synaptic Transmission: The Ion Channels That Feel Like Thought

Nicotinic acetylcholine receptors are pentameric ligand-gated ion channels that allow sodium influx when acetylcholine binds, depolarizing the muscle fiber. Glutamate activates two main receptor types in the brain: AMPA receptors produce fast excitatory currents, while NMDA receptors are blocked by magnesium at the resting membrane potential and only conduct when the cell is depolarized. NMDA receptors detect coincident presynaptic activity (glutamate binding) and postsynaptic depolarization, providing the biophysical basis for Hebbian plasticity. GABAA and glycine receptors conduct chloride to hyperpolarize neurons. Benzodiazepines enhance GABAA function, while ketamine blocks NMDA receptors. Current magnitude follows Ohm's law: I = g(V − Erev), where excitatory currents reverse near 0 mV and inhibitory currents reverse at the chloride equilibrium potential. NMDA receptors require both glutamate and a co-agonist (glycine or D-serine) to function.

Neural circuits differ fundamentally from electronic circuits in several ways. Neurons perform analog integration of synaptic currents with variable time constants and nonlinear dendritic processing. Action potentials are discrete, threshold-driven events that encode information in both firing rates and precise timing. Synapses have probabilistic release and exhibit short-term plasticity (facilitation and depression) and long-term plasticity that modifies connection strength. Neuromodulators alter both intrinsic neuronal properties and synaptic transmission rules. Astrocytes and extracellular ion concentrations further modify circuit behavior. Unlike deterministic digital circuits that operate on synchronized clock cycles, neural networks are asynchronous, history-dependent, and inherently noisy, using this variability to achieve robust computation with low energy consumption.

Acetylcholine Beyond Muscle: Attention's Whisper

Brain acetylcholine (ACh) acts through two receptor types with different functions. Nicotinic receptors are fast ligand-gated ion channels that enhance thalamocortical transmission and attention. Muscarinic receptors are G protein–coupled receptors that modulate neural network states for learning and sleep–wake cycles. Alzheimer's disease involves degeneration of basal forebrain cholinergic neurons; acetylcholinesterase inhibitors slow cognitive decline by increasing available acetylcholine. Nicotine addiction involves upregulation of nicotinic receptors. Atropine blocks muscarinic receptors, causing pupil dilation and increased heart rate. Organophosphate compounds irreversibly inhibit acetylcholinesterase, causing acetylcholine accumulation and potentially fatal respiratory paralysis; treatment requires atropine plus pralidoxime to reactivate the enzyme.

Biogenic Amines: The Mood, Drive, and Arousal Triad

Dopamine was first studied in Parkinson's disease, where L-DOPA treatment restored movement by replenishing dopamine. Wolfram Schultz's recordings revealed that dopamine neurons encode prediction errors: they fire when rewards exceed expectations but not for fully predicted rewards. This teaches the brain about reward contingencies. Drugs of abuse artificially activate this learning signal. Antipsychotic medications block dopamine D2 receptors to reduce psychotic symptoms but can cause movement side effects. The "chemical imbalance" theory oversimplified psychiatric disorders by focusing on single neurotransmitters rather than complex network dysfunction.

Norepinephrine released from the locus coeruleus regulates arousal and attention. It causes pupil dilation and shifts cortical networks from exploratory to focused processing modes. Beta-adrenergic blockers reduce norepinephrine effects, decreasing anxiety and tremor. SNRIs (serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors) treat depression by increasing synaptic norepinephrine. ADHD medications often target norepinephrine systems to improve prefrontal cortex function and attention.

Serotonin is an ancient signaling molecule found in organisms without nervous systems. SSRIs increase synaptic serotonin within hours but therapeutic effects emerge over weeks, suggesting that neuroplasticity changes rather than acute neurotransmitter levels drive clinical improvement. Psychedelic drugs that activate 5-HT2A receptors can disrupt rigid neural network patterns. Current research investigates whether psychedelic-assisted therapy can produce lasting therapeutic changes in treatment-resistant psychiatric conditions, though this remains an active area of clinical investigation.

Amino Acid Transmitters: The Brain's Workhorses

Glutamate is the brain's primary excitatory neurotransmitter. Excessive glutamate release causes excitotoxicity through calcium overload, contributing to stroke and traumatic brain injury. AMPA receptors mediate fast excitatory transmission while NMDA receptors allow calcium entry that triggers plasticity. GABA is the main inhibitory neurotransmitter, synthesized from glutamate by glutamic acid decarboxylase (GAD). GABAA receptors conduct chloride for fast inhibition while GABAB receptors are metabotropic and produce slower effects. Glycine provides inhibition in spinal cord and brainstem circuits; strychnine blocks glycine receptors, causing fatal convulsions that demonstrate inhibition's essential role.

Neurotransmitter receptors are major drug targets: benzodiazepines, barbiturates, and zolpidem enhance GABAA function; ketamine and dextromethorphan block NMDA receptors; memantine provides partial NMDA blockade in dementia treatment. These drugs demonstrate that neural computation depends on precise timing and probabilistic signaling rather than simple on/off switches.

GABAA receptors often produce "shunting inhibition" by increasing chloride conductance, which clamps membrane potential near the chloride equilibrium potential and reduces the impact of excitatory inputs without necessarily causing large hyperpolarization. Chloride homeostasis determines GABA's effect: early in development, NKCC1 transporters maintain high intracellular chloride so GABA is depolarizing, while mature neurons express KCC2 transporters that extrude chloride, making GABA hyperpolarizing. Pathological shifts in chloride gradients can reverse GABA's effect from inhibitory to excitatory, contributing to epilepsy and chronic pain.

Clinically Speaking: When Synapses Go Sideways

Myasthenia gravis: antibodies attack nicotinic ACh receptors—drooping eyelids by afternoon, strength returns with rest; acetylcholinesterase inhibitors and immune therapy restore the conversation.

Botulism: contaminated honey or canned foods; SNAREs clipped; ACh silenced; ICU ventilators as bridge back to breath. Therapeutic Botox is the same toxin wielded with a jeweler's hand.

Tetanus: toxin rides retrograde, disarms inhibitory interneurons, and leaves motor neurons unopposed—lockjaw is disinhibition written in muscle.

Excitotoxic stroke: glutamate floods, NMDA overopens, calcium cascades; cooling the brain buys neurons time.
